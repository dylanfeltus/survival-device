# Fire Starting Techniques

*Original content based on US Army FM 21-76/FM 3-05.70 principles (public domain)*

## Importance of Fire

Fire provides warmth, water purification, cooking, signaling, predator deterrent, and psychological comfort. Mastering multiple fire-starting methods is essential for survival.

## Fire Requirements

**The Fire Triangle:**
1. **Heat** — ignition source
2. **Fuel** — material that burns
3. **Oxygen** — air flow

Remove any element and fire fails. All three must be balanced.

## Fuel Preparation

**Tinder** (catches spark easily):
- Dry grass, leaves, or moss
- Birch bark (peels easily, burns even when wet)
- Cotton balls, lint, or char cloth
- Fine wood shavings
- Pine needles
- Fatwood or resinous wood

**Kindling** (small sticks, finger-width):
- Dead twigs still on trees (drier than ground wood)
- Pencil-to-thumb thickness
- Split wood from larger pieces

**Fuel Wood** (sustained burning):
- Arm-thickness and larger
- Dead standing wood is driest
- Split wood burns better than rounds
- Hardwoods burn longer than softwoods

**Test for Dryness:** Dead wood should snap cleanly, not bend.

## Fire Starting Methods

### 1. Friction Methods

**Bow Drill:**

*Components needed:*
- Bow (curved branch with cordage)
- Drill (straight stick, 6-8" long, 3/4" diameter)
- Fireboard (flat softwood piece)
- Handhold (protects top hand)
- Tinder bundle

*Procedure:*
1. Cut V-notch in fireboard where drill will spin
2. Place tinder bundle under notch
3. Loop bow string around drill once
4. Place drill in notch, hold handhold on top
5. Bow back and forth rapidly with steady downward pressure
6. Smoke will appear, then glowing ember in notch
7. Carefully transfer ember to tinder bundle
8. Blow gently to create flame

**Efficiency:** High effort, but reliable with practice.

**Hand Drill** (similar but uses hands instead of bow):
- More difficult, requires faster spinning
- Best with very dry softwood and optimal tinder

### 2. Flint and Steel / Ferro Rod

**Procedure:**
1. Prepare tinder bundle first
2. Hold striker at 45° angle
3. Strike down length of ferro rod with force
4. Direct sparks into tinder
5. Blow gently when smoke appears

**Tips:**
- Strike the steel/striker, not the rod
- Char cloth catches sparks more easily
- Keep rod dry and scrape oxidation off before use

**Advantage:** Works in all weather, nearly unlimited uses.

### 3. Lens Method

**Using Magnifying Glass or Clear Water:**

*Procedure:*
1. Create tinder bundle with finest material possible
2. Focus sunlight to smallest, brightest point on tinder
3. Hold steady (30 seconds to 2 minutes)
4. Smoke will appear, then ember
5. Blow gently to create flame

**Improvised Lenses:**
- Bottom of clear plastic or glass bottle filled with water
- Ice shaped into lens (smooth and clear)
- Eyeglasses or camera lens

**Limitation:** Requires direct sunlight, doesn't work at night or in overcast conditions.

### 4. Battery and Steel Wool

**Procedure:**
1. Stretch steel wool to thin strand
2. Touch both battery terminals to steel wool simultaneously
3. Steel wool ignites immediately
4. Transfer to tinder bundle quickly

**Works with:** 9V battery, AA battery (touch terminals together with steel wool bridge), car battery.

**WARNING:** Produces very hot, fast flame. Have tinder ready before starting.

### 5. Matches and Lighters

**Best Practices:**
- Store in waterproof container
- Strike-anywhere matches are most versatile
- Lighters fail in extreme cold (warm in hands first)
- Always carry backup method

## Fire Types

**Tepee Fire:**
- Best for: Quick heat, boiling water
- Arrangement: Tinder in center, kindling/fuel in cone shape
- Burns hot and fast

**Log Cabin Fire:**
- Best for: Cooking, sustained heat
- Arrangement: Square layers of progressively smaller wood
- Burns evenly and long

**Star Fire:**
- Best for: Fuel conservation
- Arrangement: Logs radiate out from center like star points
- Push logs inward as they burn

**Signal Fire:**
- Add green vegetation or wet materials to create thick white smoke
- Build large to be visible from distance
- Prepare three fires in triangle pattern (international distress signal)

## Fire Safety

**Site Selection:**
- Clear 10-foot diameter down to bare soil
- Away from overhanging branches
- Sheltered from wind but with ventilation
- Never inside enclosed shelter without proper chimney

**Weather Considerations:**
- In wind: dig fire pit or build windbreak
- In rain: build platform or use overhang
- In snow: build platform of green logs

**Extinguishing:**
1. Separate burning materials
2. Drown with water
3. Stir ashes and add more water
4. Feel with back of hand to ensure cool
5. Cover with dirt

**WARNING:** Forest fires can start from seemingly dead coals. Always ensure fire is completely out before leaving.

## Fire in Survival

A fire can save your life through warmth and water purification, but it can also attract unwanted attention or start a wildfire. Always weigh the risks versus benefits based on your specific situation.

**Priority Fire Uses:**
1. Hypothermia treatment (life-threatening)
2. Water purification (critical within 72 hours)
3. Signaling rescue
4. Cooking and morale
5. Predator deterrent

Master at least two fire-starting methods — one primitive, one modern. Practice before you need them.
