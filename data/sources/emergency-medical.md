# Emergency Medical Situations

*Original content based on US Army FM 21-76/FM 3-05.70 principles (public domain)*

## Life-Threatening Medical Emergencies

Some injuries and medical conditions require immediate action. In survival situations, quick recognition and treatment can prevent death.

## Allergic Reactions / Anaphylaxis

**Severe allergic reactions can kill in minutes.**

### Signs and Symptoms

**Mild to Moderate:**
- Hives, itching, rash
- Swelling of lips, tongue, or face
- Nausea or vomiting

**Severe (Anaphylaxis):**
- Difficulty breathing, wheezing
- Swelling of throat/tongue blocking airway
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Dizziness or loss of consciousness
- Blue lips or fingernails

### Treatment

**If epinephrine auto-injector (EpiPen) available:**
1. Remove from packaging
2. Hold firmly against outer thigh (can inject through clothing)
3. Press and hold for 3 seconds
4. Massage injection area for 10 seconds
5. Keep person lying down
6. Second dose may be needed after 5-15 minutes if no improvement

**Without epinephrine:**
1. Help person sit in position easiest for breathing
2. If person stops breathing, begin CPR
3. Evacuate immediately — this is life-threatening

**Prevention:** If known allergy, avoid triggers. Carry epinephrine if prescribed.

## Snake and Spider Bites

### Venomous Snake Bite

**Signs:**
- Puncture marks (one or two)
- Immediate severe pain and burning
- Swelling spreading from bite
- Nausea, vomiting
- Difficulty breathing
- Blurred vision

**Treatment:**
1. Get away from snake (avoid second bite)
2. Keep person calm and still (movement spreads venom)
3. Remove jewelry/tight clothing before swelling starts
4. Position bite below heart level if possible
5. Clean wound gently
6. Immobilize bitten limb with splint
7. Mark edge of swelling with pen, note time
8. Evacuate to medical care immediately

**DO NOT:**
- Cut the bite or try to suck venom
- Apply ice or tourniquet
- Give alcohol or stimulants
- Waste time trying to catch/kill snake

**Note:** Most snake bites are not fatal if treated properly. Stay calm, immobilize, evacuate.

### Spider Bites (Black Widow, Brown Recluse)

**Black Widow Signs:**
- Immediate sharp pain
- Muscle cramps and rigidity
- Nausea, vomiting
- Difficulty breathing

**Brown Recluse Signs:**
- Initially painless
- After hours: blister forms at bite site
- Tissue death (necrosis) spreading from bite
- Fever, chills, nausea

**Treatment:**
1. Clean wound
2. Apply cool compress
3. Elevate bite area
4. Give pain relievers if available
5. Monitor for worsening symptoms
6. Seek medical care (antivenom exists for black widow)

## Broken Bones (Fractures)

### Open Fracture (Bone Through Skin)

**Extreme emergency** — risk of infection and severe bleeding.

**Treatment:**
1. Control bleeding (direct pressure on wound, not on bone)
2. Do NOT push bone back in
3. Cover wound with sterile dressing
4. Immobilize with splint as is
5. Treat for shock
6. Evacuate immediately

### Closed Fracture

**Treatment:**
1. Immobilize injured area
2. Splint above and below fracture
3. Check circulation before and after splinting
4. Apply ice if available (reduce swelling)
5. Elevate if possible
6. Give pain medication if available

**Circulation Check:**
- Press fingernail or toe (turns white)
- Release and watch for color return (should return within 2 seconds)
- Check warmth and sensation

## Hypothermia

**Body temperature drops below 95°F / 35°C.**

### Progressive Symptoms

**Mild (95-90°F):**
- Shivering (intense)
- Confusion, difficulty speaking
- Fumbling hands, stumbling
- Drowsiness

**Moderate (90-82°F):**
- Violent shivering, then stops
- Irrational behavior, paradoxical undressing
- Muscle rigidity
- Slow, shallow breathing
- Weak pulse

**Severe (below 82°F):**
- Unconsciousness
- Barely detectable pulse/breathing
- Rigid muscles
- Appears dead

### Treatment

**Mild Hypothermia:**
1. Get out of cold/wet environment
2. Remove wet clothing
3. Insulate from ground
4. Wrap in dry blankets/sleeping bags
5. Give warm, sweet drinks (if conscious and able to swallow)
6. Skin-to-skin contact in sleeping bag (rescuer and victim)

**Severe Hypothermia:**
1. Handle extremely gently (rough handling can cause cardiac arrest)
2. Don't assume person is dead (they may be revivable)
3. Insulate from further heat loss
4. Rewarm core only (chest, neck, groin) — not extremities
5. If breathing/pulse absent, begin CPR and continue until warm
6. Evacuate with extreme care

**DO NOT:**
- Rub or massage skin
- Give alcohol
- Warm too quickly (causes shock)
- Apply heat to arms/legs (can cause heart failure)

**Critical Rule:** "Nobody is dead until they're warm and dead."

## Heat Stroke

**Life-threatening. Body temperature above 104°F / 40°C.**

### Signs and Symptoms

- High body temperature
- Hot, red, dry skin (or heavy sweating)
- Rapid, strong pulse
- Confusion, altered mental state
- Seizures
- Unconsciousness

### Treatment

**This is a medical emergency:**

1. Move to shade/cool environment immediately
2. Remove excess clothing
3. Cool aggressively:
   - Spray/pour cool water on skin
   - Fan to increase evaporation
   - Ice packs on neck, armpits, groin
   - Immerse in cool water if available
4. Continue cooling until body temperature drops to 102°F / 39°C
5. If conscious, give cool water to drink slowly
6. Monitor breathing and pulse
7. Evacuate immediately

**DO NOT:**
- Give alcohol or caffeine
- Give salt tablets
- Use ice water (too cold, can cause shock)

## Drowning / Near-Drowning

### Response

1. Ensure scene safety (don't become second victim)
2. Remove from water
3. Check breathing and pulse
4. If not breathing, begin CPR immediately (don't wait to drain water)
5. Continue CPR until breathing resumes or professional help arrives
6. Keep warm (hypothermia common in drowning)
7. Seek medical care even if person recovers (secondary drowning possible hours later)

**Note:** People can drown in as little as 2 inches of water. Unconscious persons face-down in any water depth are drowning emergencies.

## Severe Bleeding (Arterial)

**Arterial bleeding is pulsing, bright red, and rapidly fatal.**

### Treatment Priority

1. Direct pressure on wound
2. Elevate wounded limb above heart
3. Pressure point (artery against bone upstream from wound)
4. Tourniquet (if limb and bleeding cannot be controlled otherwise)

**See First Aid Basics document for detailed bleeding control procedures.**

## When to Evacuate Immediately

- Altered mental status or unconsciousness
- Difficulty breathing
- Severe bleeding not controlled
- Suspected spinal injury
- Open fractures
- Chest or abdominal injuries
- Anaphylaxis
- Heat stroke or severe hypothermia
- Severe burns
- Venomous bites

**Remember:** Survival medicine is about preventing death and stabilizing until professional care is available. Know your limits and evacuate when injuries exceed your capability to treat.
