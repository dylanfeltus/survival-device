# First Aid Basics

*Original content based on US Army FM 21-76/FM 3-05.70 principles (public domain)*

## Survival First Aid Priorities

In survival situations, medical care focuses on preventing life-threatening conditions and stabilizing injuries until rescue. Know your limits — some injuries require professional medical care.

## Assessment: ABC Method

Always check in this order:
1. **Airway** — Is it clear? Can they breathe?
2. **Breathing** — Are they breathing adequately?
3. **Circulation** — Is there severe bleeding? Do they have a pulse?

**Don't move injured person unless:**
- Immediate danger (fire, flooding, combat zone)
- Need to perform CPR (must be on firm, flat surface)
- Location prevents proper treatment

## Severe Bleeding Control

**Life-threatening bleeding kills in minutes.** This is your highest medical priority.

### Direct Pressure

**Procedure:**
1. Expose wound (cut away clothing if needed)
2. Apply sterile dressing or cleanest cloth available
3. Press firmly directly on wound
4. Maintain pressure for 5-10 minutes minimum
5. If blood soaks through, add more dressings on top (don't remove first one)
6. Bandage firmly in place once bleeding stops

### Pressure Points

If direct pressure fails, apply pressure to artery supplying that limb:
- **Arm:** Brachial artery (inside of upper arm)
- **Leg:** Femoral artery (groin area)

Press artery against bone while maintaining direct pressure on wound.

### Tourniquet (Last Resort)

**Only use for:**
- Severe limb bleeding not controlled by pressure
- Multiple casualties (allows you to treat others)
- Amputation or partial amputation

**Procedure:**
1. Place tourniquet 2-3 inches above wound, never on joint
2. Tighten until bleeding stops completely
3. Mark time applied on tourniquet (use blood if necessary)
4. Do NOT loosen or remove once applied
5. Seek immediate medical evacuation

**WARNING:** Tourniquets can result in limb loss. Only use when death from bleeding is imminent.

## Wound Care

**Cleaning:**
1. Wash hands or use cleanest water available
2. Irrigate wound with clean/purified water
3. Remove visible debris gently
4. Pat dry with clean cloth

**Dressing:**
1. Apply antibiotic ointment if available
2. Cover with sterile dressing
3. Bandage firmly but not so tight it cuts circulation
4. Check circulation below wound (color, warmth, sensation)
5. Change dressing daily and inspect for infection

**Infection Signs:**
- Increasing redness spreading from wound
- Swelling
- Heat
- Pus or foul odor
- Red streaks running from wound
- Fever

**Action:** Keep wound clean and dry. If infected, increase dressing changes and seek medical help urgently.

## Fractures and Splinting

**Fracture Indicators:**
- Severe pain at injury site
- Deformity or unnatural angle
- Inability to use limb
- Swelling and bruising
- Grating sensation (crepitus)

### Splinting Procedure

**Principles:**
- Immobilize joint above and below fracture
- Pad splint material (prevent pressure sores)
- Check circulation before and after splinting
- If limb is blue or without pulse, gently try to align — this is life-over-limb situation

**Improvised Splints:**
- Straight sticks or branches
- Rolled newspapers or magazines
- Cardboard
- Internal splinting (tape injured finger to adjacent finger)

**Application:**
1. Position limb as close to natural position as tolerated
2. Place padded splints on both sides of fracture
3. Tie above and below fracture, not on fracture site
4. Check circulation (color, warmth, can they feel touch?)

## CPR Basics

**When to Perform:**
- Person not breathing
- No pulse

**Adult CPR:**
1. Call for help / prepare for evacuation
2. Place person on firm, flat surface
3. Position hands center of chest (lower half of breastbone)
4. Push hard and fast: 2 inches deep, 100-120 compressions per minute
5. After 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths (if trained)
6. Continue 30:2 ratio until person breathes or help arrives

**Compression-Only CPR:** If untrained in rescue breathing, continuous chest compressions alone still saves lives.

**Don't stop** until:
- Person starts breathing
- Professional help arrives
- You're too exhausted to continue
- Scene becomes unsafe

## Shock Treatment

**Shock Signs:**
- Pale, cool, clammy skin
- Rapid, weak pulse
- Rapid breathing
- Anxiety, confusion
- Weakness or dizziness

**Treatment:**
1. Treat underlying cause (stop bleeding, etc.)
2. Lay person flat (or position for comfort if chest injury)
3. Elevate legs 12 inches (if no spinal injury)
4. Maintain body temperature (prevent heat loss)
5. Loosen restrictive clothing
6. Do NOT give food or drink
7. Reassure and keep calm

## Heat Injuries

### Heat Exhaustion

**Signs:** Heavy sweating, weakness, nausea, headache, pale/clammy skin.

**Treatment:**
1. Move to shade
2. Remove excess clothing
3. Cool with water (drink if conscious, wet skin if not)
4. Rest with legs elevated

### Heat Stroke (Life-Threatening)

**Signs:** High body temperature, red/hot/dry skin, rapid pulse, confusion, unconsciousness.

**Treatment:**
1. Emergency evacuation needed immediately
2. Move to shade
3. Remove clothing
4. Cool aggressively (water, fanning, ice packs on neck/armpits/groin)
5. Continue cooling until body temperature drops

## Cold Injuries

### Hypothermia

**Mild Signs:** Shivering, confusion, fumbling hands, slurred speech.

**Severe Signs:** No shivering, rigid muscles, barely conscious, weak pulse.

**Treatment:**
1. Get out of cold/wet environment
2. Remove wet clothing
3. Insulate from ground
4. Wrap in dry blankets/sleeping bag
5. Apply heat sources (fire, warm water bottles to core — chest, neck, groin)
6. Give warm, sweet drinks if conscious
7. Do NOT rub skin or give alcohol

**WARNING:** Severe hypothermia victims seem dead but may be revived. Handle gently, rewarm slowly.

### Frostbite

**Signs:** White or grayish-yellow skin, numbness, hard/waxy skin texture.

**Treatment:**
1. Do NOT thaw if there's risk of refreezing (causes severe damage)
2. Remove wet/constrictive clothing
3. If safe to thaw: immerse in warm water (98-102°F / 37-39°C)
4. Do NOT rub or massage
5. Separate affected digits with gauze
6. Protect from further cold

## Dehydration

**Signs:**
- Dark urine or decreased urination
- Dry mouth and lips
- Dizziness
- Rapid heartbeat
- Sunken eyes

**Treatment:**
- Drink water slowly and steadily
- Add pinch of salt and sugar to aid absorption
- Rest in shade
- If severe, medical evacuation needed

## Survival First Aid Kit Essentials

**Minimum:**
- Sterile gauze pads
- Adhesive bandages (various sizes)
- Elastic bandage
- Trauma dressing
- Antiseptic wipes
- Antibiotic ointment
- Pain relievers
- Tourniquet
- Medical tape
- Scissors/knife

**Remember:** Knowledge is more important than equipment. You can improvise materials, but you cannot improvise knowledge.

## Legal and Ethical Considerations

**Good Samaritan Principle:** In emergencies, render aid to the best of your ability. Document what you do and when.

**Limits:** Know when an injury exceeds your capability. Stabilize and evacuate rather than attempt procedures beyond your training.
