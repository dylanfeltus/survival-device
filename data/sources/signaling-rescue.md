# Signaling and Rescue

*Original content based on US Army FM 21-76/FM 3-05.70 principles (public domain)*

## Signaling Principles

Being found requires being seen or heard. Effective signaling can cut days off your survival ordeal.

**Key Principles:**
- **Contrast:** Signal must stand out from environment
- **Movement:** Attracts attention better than static displays
- **Repetition:** Signal continuously or at intervals
- **Location:** Signal from highest, most visible point safe to reach
- **Preparation:** Have signals ready before you see/hear rescue

## Visual Signals

### Signal Fire

**Three fires in triangle pattern** = international distress signal (each fire 25-30 meters apart).

**Day Signals (Smoke):**
- Add green vegetation, rubber, or oil to create dark smoke
- White smoke shows well against dark forest
- Black smoke shows well against snow or light terrain
- Keep fire smoldering, not flaming

**Night Signals (Flame):**
- Build fire on high point visible from distance
- Keep flames bright and large
- Three fires still more recognizable than one

**Maintenance:**
- Keep fuel ready to add when aircraft heard
- Have tinder ready to restart if needed
- Position fires in clearing, not under trees

### Signal Mirror

**Most effective daytime signal** — visible for miles.

**Procedure:**
1. Hold mirror near face
2. Extend other hand toward target (aircraft, boat, distant rescuer)
3. Reflect sunlight onto extended hand
4. Adjust mirror angle to "bounce" light from hand to target
5. Flash repeatedly (SOS pattern if possible)

**Improvised Mirrors:**
- Any reflective surface (CD, phone screen, belt buckle, polished can bottom)
- Even non-perfect reflection can be visible from miles away

### Ground-to-Air Signals

**Create large symbols visible from aircraft:**

**SOS** — Universal distress (three short, three long, three short)

**Other Signals:**
- **X** = Need medical assistance
- **F** = Need food and water
- **II** = Need medical supplies
- **→** = Proceeding in this direction

**Construction:**
- Each line 3-6 meters wide, 12+ meters long
- Use contrasting materials (rocks on snow, logs on grass, cleared areas in vegetation)
- Position in open area visible from multiple directions

### Panel Signals

**Orange or bright colored material:**
- Wave clothing or fabric
- Lay panels in X or SOS pattern on ground
- Attach to poles and wave

**VS-17 Panel** (military signal panel):
- Orange on one side, pink/violet on other
- Highly visible against most backgrounds

## Audio Signals

### Whistle

**Advantages:** Carries farther than voice, requires less energy, doesn't damage throat.

**Pattern:**
- Three blasts = universal distress
- Repeat at intervals

**Range:** Up to 1 mile in open terrain, less in forest.

### Shouting

**Effective but exhausting:**
- Cup hands around mouth
- Shout toward likely rescuer direction
- Three shouts, pause, repeat

**Preserve voice** — use whistle when possible.

### Noise Makers

- Bang metal objects together
- Strike hollow logs
- Use any available sound-producing method

## Electronic Signals

### Cell Phone

**Even without service:**
- Keep battery charged
- Turn off when not signaling to conserve power
- Move to high ground to check signal
- Send text messages (require less signal than calls)
- Use phone flashlight for signaling at night (SOS pattern)

### Personal Locator Beacon (PLB) / Emergency Position Indicating Radio Beacon (EPIRB)

**If you have one:**
- Activate only in genuine emergency
- Transmits location to rescue satellites
- Keep dry and protect from damage
- Don't turn off once activated until rescue arrives

## SOS Pattern

**International Distress Signal:**

Three short, three long, three short (· · · — — — · · ·)

**Applications:**
- Whistle blasts
- Light flashes
- Sound signals
- Ground patterns

**Morse Code SOS timing:**
- Short = 1 count
- Long = 3 counts
- Pause between signals = 1 count
- Pause between letters = 3 counts

## Day vs. Night Strategies

**Daytime:**
- Signal mirror (most effective)
- Smoke signals
- Ground panels
- Waving bright materials

**Nighttime:**
- Fire (bright flames)
- Flashlight or improvised torch
- Reflections from fire on mirrors/reflective surfaces
- Chemical light sticks if available

## Signaling Aircraft

**When you hear aircraft:**

1. **Move to clearing immediately**
2. **Activate signal** (mirror, fire, panel)
3. **Wave bright materials** with large motions
4. **Stand in open** where you're visible
5. **Continue signaling** until aircraft clearly acknowledges or passes

**Aircraft Acknowledgment:**
- Rocking wings = message received
- Circling = you've been spotted
- Green flare/light = proceed as signaled
- Red flare/light = await further signals

## Body Signals to Helicopters

**Spread eagle (Y shape):** YES — need pickup

**Arms down in V shape:** NO — don't need pickup

**Both arms raised straight up:** Pick up here

**One arm up, one down (diagonal):** Need medical assistance

**Waving with both arms:** Can proceed on own, don't need assistance

## Signaling Strategy

**Priority:**
1. Stay visible and in open areas when possible
2. Keep signals ready to activate instantly
3. Signal continuously once rescuers are in area
4. Have backup signals prepared

**Remember:** Rescue depends on being found. Invest energy in effective signaling. One successful signal can save days or weeks of survival struggle.
