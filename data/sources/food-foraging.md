# Food Foraging and Procurement

*Original content based on US Army FM 21-76/FM 3-05.70 principles (public domain)*

## Food in Survival Situations

The human body can survive weeks without food but only days without water. **Food is rarely your first priority** — shelter, water, and signaling typically come first. However, prolonged survival situations require caloric intake.

**Energy Economics:** Foraging burns calories. Don't expend more energy obtaining food than the food provides.

## Universal Edibility Test

**CRITICAL:** Only use when you cannot identify a plant with certainty and no other food is available. This test takes 24+ hours.

**Never test:**
- Mushrooms or fungi (too dangerous)
- Plants with milky or discolored sap
- Plants with beans, bulbs, or seeds inside pods
- Plants with thorns, fine hairs, or spines
- Plants with dill, carrot, parsnip, or parsley-like foliage (many are poisonous)
- Plants with three-leaved growth pattern (potential poison ivy)

**Procedure:**

1. **Separate:** Test only one part of plant at a time (leaf, stem, root)
2. **Smell:** Crush and smell. Discard if strong or acid odor
3. **Skin Contact:** Rub plant juice on inside of elbow. Wait 15 minutes. If burning, itching, or rash appears, discard
4. **Lip Test:** Place small amount on outer lip. Wait 3 minutes. Discard if burning or irritation
5. **Tongue Test:** Place on tongue, hold for 15 minutes. Don't swallow. Discard if burning or irritation
6. **Chew Test:** Chew small amount for 15 minutes. Don't swallow. Discard if burning or irritation
7. **Swallow Test:** Swallow the chewed portion. Wait 8 hours. Eat and drink nothing else during this time
8. **Monitor:** If nausea, vomiting, cramps, or diarrhea occur, induce vomiting and drink plenty of water. Plant is not edible
9. **Eating Test:** If no ill effects, eat 1/4 cup of same plant part. Wait 8 hours
10. **Confirmation:** If no ill effects, plant part is safe to eat

**Cook all wild plants when possible** — cooking destroys some toxins.

## Edible Plant Categories (General Principles)

**WARNING:** Proper plant identification requires detailed knowledge and ideally field guides with pictures. These are general categories only.

### Generally Safer Categories

**Berries:**
- Blue/black berries = usually safe
- White/yellow berries = usually poisonous
- Red berries = 50/50 — many safe, many poisonous
- **Always test unknown berries using edibility test**

**Nuts and Acorns:**
- Most nuts are edible
- Acorns require leaching (soak in water, change water until not bitter)

**Grasses:**
- Most grass seeds are edible when cooked
- Avoid ergot (black/purple fungus on grain heads — poisonous)

**Roots and Tubers:**
- Many are edible when cooked
- Must positively identify or use edibility test

### Common Edible Plants (Wide Geographic Distribution)

**Dandelion:** Entire plant edible (leaves, root, flowers). Young leaves less bitter.

**Cattails:** Roots, shoots, and pollen edible. Young shoots taste like cucumber.

**Clover:** Entire plant edible. Flowers and seeds most nutritious.

**Plantain:** Leaves edible raw or cooked (not banana plantain — the common lawn weed).

**Pine:** Inner bark (cambium layer) edible. Pine needle tea high in vitamin C.

**CRITICAL:** Always use reliable field guides for positive identification. These examples are for educational purposes — misidentification can be fatal.

## Trapping and Hunting

### Simple Snare

**Principle:** Create loop that tightens when animal passes through.

**Materials:**
- Wire, cord, or strong plant fiber
- Anchor (stake or fixed object)

**Placement:**
- Game trails (look for tracks, droppings, fur)
- Burrow entrances
- Fence crossings

**Legal Note:** Check local regulations. Snaring may be illegal except in survival emergencies.

### Figure-4 Deadfall Trap

**Concept:** Weight (rock/log) held up by trigger, falls when animal disturbs bait.

**Components:**
- Vertical stick (upright)
- Diagonal stick (connects vertical to horizontal)
- Horizontal stick (holds bait)
- Heavy weight

**Set on small game trails.** Practice construction before emergency.

## Fishing Without Gear

**Improvised Hooks:**
- Carved bone or wood shaped into hook
- Thorns from plants
- Safety pins bent into hooks

**Gorge Hook:**
- Small sharp stick, sharpened at both ends
- Tie line to middle
- Hide in bait
- When fish swallows, stick turns sideways and lodges

**Spearing:**
- Use straight sapling, sharpen end or split into multiple prongs
- Fish in shallow water
- Aim low (water refracts light, fish is deeper than appears)

**Trapping:**
- Build rock/stick funnel in stream (wide mouth narrows to trap)
- Check tidal pools after tide goes out

## Insects and Invertebrates

**High protein, easy to catch, abundant.**

**Generally Safe:**
- Grasshoppers/crickets (remove wings and legs)
- Grubs and larvae (found in rotting logs)
- Earthworms (purge in water for 24 hours)
- Ants (most species edible, can be sour)

**Avoid:**
- Brightly colored insects (often poisonous)
- Insects with fine hairs
- Disease-carrying insects (flies, mosquitoes, ticks)
- Spiders, scorpions, centipedes

**Preparation:** Cook when possible. Remove wings, legs, stingers.

## Food Preservation

**Drying (Jerky):**
1. Cut meat into thin strips
2. Hang in sun or near fire (not in smoke)
3. Dry until hard and brittle
4. Store in dry location

**Smoking:**
1. Build slow, cool smoke fire (no flame)
2. Hang meat above smoke
3. Smoke for 24-48 hours

**Cold Storage:**
- Bury in snow
- Submerge in cold stream (secure in bag)
- Hang in shade, away from scavengers

## Foraging Safety Rules

1. **Never eat unknown plants** without testing or expert knowledge
2. **Avoid mushrooms entirely** unless you're an expert
3. **Cook everything possible**
4. **Start with small amounts** even with "safe" foods
5. **Note what you eat** so if you get sick, you know cause
6. **Don't eat moldy, slimy, or foul-smelling food** (desperation is not worth death)

**Remember:** You can survive weeks without food. Don't risk poisoning yourself for a single meal. When in doubt, don't eat it.
