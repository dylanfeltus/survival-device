# Emergency Shelter Building

*Original content based on US Army FM 21-76/FM 3-05.70 principles (public domain)*

## Shelter Priority

Shelter is often your first priority in survival situations. Exposure to elements kills faster than hunger or thirst. A good shelter maintains body temperature and protects from wind, rain, snow, and sun.

## Site Selection

**Ideal Location:**
- Dry ground, slightly elevated to avoid water pooling
- Natural windbreaks (rocks, dense vegetation)
- Near water source but above flood line
- Avoid valley bottoms (cold air sinks)
- Access to building materials
- Visible to rescuers if desired

**Avoid:**
- Dry riverbeds (flash flood risk)
- Directly under dead trees or loose rocks
- Animal trails or dens
- Low spots that collect water
- Avalanche or rockfall zones
- Insect nests (check trees and ground)

## Insulation Principles

**Heat Loss Prevention:**
- Ground draws heat faster than air — insulate underneath first
- Layer materials for air pockets (better insulation)
- Smaller enclosed spaces retain heat better
- Block wind completely
- Keep shelter entrance at 90° angle to prevailing wind

**Insulation Materials:**
- Pine needles, leaves, moss, grass
- Bark pieces
- Snow (excellent insulator when compacted)
- Evergreen boughs
- Any dry, dead vegetation

**Rule of Thumb:** Insulation should be at least 6 inches thick on all sides, 12+ inches in extreme cold.

## Shelter Types

### 1. Lean-To Shelter

**Best Use:** Short-term, mild weather, when you have limited time.

**Construction:**
1. Find or place sturdy horizontal ridge pole between two trees (waist to chest height)
2. Lean branches at 45° angle against ridge pole
3. Layer smaller branches and vegetation over frame
4. Add thick layer of leaves, grass, or bark for waterproofing
5. Build up from bottom to top (like shingles)
6. Insulate floor with thick bed of dry vegetation

**Advantage:** Quick to build, easy to add fire reflector wall opposite opening.

**Disadvantage:** Only protects from one direction, less heat retention.

### 2. Debris Hut

**Best Use:** Cold weather, limited materials, solo survival.

**Construction:**
1. Create ridgepole from long branch, one end on ground, other supported 3-4 feet high
2. Place ribs (branches) on both sides at 45° angle
3. Fill framework with debris (leaves, grass, pine needles) 2-3 feet thick
4. Cover with layer of sticks to hold debris in place
5. Stuff interior completely with dry insulation material
6. Create door plug from vegetation

**Interior:** Should be just big enough to fit your body, not larger. Small space = retained body heat.

**Advantage:** Excellent insulation, uses only natural materials, warm in extreme cold.

**Disadvantage:** Time-consuming, requires large amount of debris.

### 3. Snow Cave

**Best Use:** Deep snow conditions, extreme cold, winter mountaineering.

**Construction:**
1. Find snowdrift or slope with packed snow at least 6 feet deep
2. Dig entrance tunnel upward into drift
3. Hollow out sleeping chamber above tunnel entrance level
4. Chamber ceiling should be domed (stronger, prevents drips)
5. Smooth interior ceiling to prevent dripping
6. Poke small ventilation hole through roof with stick
7. Mark exterior with branches so you can find it

**Critical:** Sleeping chamber must be higher than entrance (cold air sinks). Keep ventilation hole clear.

**Temperature:** Inside temperature typically stays near 32°F regardless of outside temperature (can be -40°F outside).

**WARNING:** Always carry digging tool. Never seal entrance completely. Carbon dioxide buildup can be fatal.

### 4. Poncho/Tarp Shelter

**Best Use:** When you have manufactured materials, need quick setup.

**A-Frame Configuration:**
1. Tie cordage between two trees at chest height
2. Drape poncho/tarp over line
3. Stake out corners at 45° angle
4. Weight edges with rocks or logs

**Enhancements:**
- Dig shallow drainage ditch around perimeter
- Add insulation layer inside
- Use additional poncho for ground cloth

**Advantage:** Fast, waterproof, lightweight materials.

**Disadvantage:** Requires manufactured materials.

### 5. Tree Pit Shelter (Winter)

**Best Use:** Deep snow, evergreen forest.

**Construction:**
1. Find large evergreen tree with low-hanging branches
2. Dig down to ground around trunk (snow is shallow near trunk)
3. Use removed snow to build up walls
4. Lay evergreen boughs on floor and over top opening
5. Leave small opening for entry and ventilation

**Advantage:** Natural insulation from tree and snow, quick to build.

## Shelter Improvements

**Fire Reflector Wall:**
- Build stone or log wall opposite shelter opening
- Reflects heat back into shelter
- Position fire 3-4 feet from entrance

**Drainage:**
- Dig shallow trench around shelter perimeter
- Prevent water from running inside during rain

**Bedding:**
- Never sleep directly on ground
- Build raised bed platform if possible
- Thick insulation layer critical (test by lying down — if ground feels cold through insulation, add more)

## Emergency Considerations

**Time Available:**
- Less than 1 hour: find natural shelter (cave, rock overhang, fallen tree)
- 1-3 hours: build basic lean-to
- 3+ hours: build debris hut or snow cave

**Physiological State:**
- If hypothermic: get out of wind immediately, any shelter better than none
- If injured: prioritize easy construction over optimal insulation

A mediocre shelter built before dark is better than a perfect shelter half-finished. Start early, build quickly, improve over time.
