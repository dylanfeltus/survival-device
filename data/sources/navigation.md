# Navigation and Orientation

*Original content based on US Army FM 21-76/FM 3-05.70 principles (public domain)*

## The Importance of Navigation

Getting lost can turn a minor survival situation into a life-threatening emergency. Understanding basic navigation prevents wasted energy, dehydration, and panic.

## Map Reading Basics

### Topographic Maps

**Contour Lines:** Show elevation changes.
- Lines close together = steep terrain
- Lines far apart = gentle slope
- Lines forming a V pointing uphill = valley/drainage
- Lines forming a V pointing downhill = ridge

**Map Legend:** Identifies symbols for roads, water, buildings, vegetation.

**Scale:** Determines distance (e.g., 1:24,000 means 1 inch = 24,000 inches on ground).

**Declination Diagram:** Shows difference between true north and magnetic north (critical for accurate compass use).

### Orient Your Map

1. Align map's north with compass north
2. Adjust for magnetic declination
3. Match terrain features (hills, water) to map symbols
4. Identify your location by triangulating known landmarks

## Compass Use

### Parts of a Compass

- **Magnetic Needle:** Always points magnetic north
- **Rotating Bezel:** Marks degrees (0-360°)
- **Direction-of-Travel Arrow:** Where you're heading
- **Orienting Lines:** Align with north on map

### Taking a Bearing

1. Point direction-of-travel arrow at landmark
2. Rotate bezel until orienting arrow aligns with magnetic needle
3. Read bearing number at index line
4. Follow direction-of-travel arrow, keeping needle aligned

### Following a Bearing

1. Set bearing on bezel
2. Hold compass flat at waist level
3. Rotate your body until magnetic needle aligns with orienting arrow
4. Walk toward direction-of-travel arrow
5. Pick intermediate landmark and walk to it
6. Repeat until destination reached

**Common Errors:**
- Metal objects nearby (belt buckles, knives) interfere with compass
- Holding compass at wrong angle
- Not adjusting for magnetic declination

## Natural Navigation (No Equipment)

### Sun Navigation

**Northern Hemisphere:**
- Sun rises in east, sets in west (approximately)
- At noon, sun is due south
- Shadows point north at solar noon

**Shadow Stick Method:**
1. Place stick vertically in ground
2. Mark tip of shadow with stone
3. Wait 15-30 minutes
4. Mark new shadow tip
5. Line between marks runs east-west (first mark is west)
6. Perpendicular line runs north-south

### Star Navigation

**Northern Hemisphere - Polaris (North Star):**
1. Find Big Dipper constellation
2. Locate two stars forming outer edge of "cup"
3. Draw imaginary line through these stars
4. Extend line about 5 times the distance
5. Bright star you reach is Polaris = true north

**Southern Hemisphere - Southern Cross:**
1. Find Southern Cross constellation
2. Draw imaginary line along long axis
3. Extend line 4.5 times its length
4. Drop line down to horizon = south

### Natural Indicators (Less Reliable)

**Moss Growth:** Common myth that moss only grows on north side is FALSE. Moss grows on the dampest side, which varies by region.

**Tree Growth:** Trees often (but not always) have fuller branches on south side (in Northern Hemisphere) due to more sunlight.

**Ant Hills:** In northern regions, often have steeper slope on north side.

**Use these only as secondary indicators, never as primary navigation.**

## Dead Reckoning

When visibility is poor or terrain featureless:

1. **Set Direction:** Choose bearing using compass or natural methods
2. **Pace Count:** Determine how many steps you take per 100 meters (count every time left foot strikes)
3. **Track Distance:** Count paces as you travel
4. **Record:** Note direction and distance traveled at each turn
5. **Plot:** Keep mental or written map of your route

**Practice your pace count:** Varies with terrain, load, fatigue. Test on known distance.

## Landmark Navigation

**Handrails:** Linear features to follow (rivers, roads, ridgelines, fences).

**Baselines:** Large features you can't miss (coastline, major highway, mountain range).

**Catching Features:** Landmark beyond destination that signals you've gone too far.

**Attack Point:** Obvious landmark near destination you navigate to first.

**Example:** To find small cabin (destination), first navigate to river (attack point), then follow river (handrail) to cabin. If you reach bridge (catching feature), you've gone too far.

## Staying Found

**Prevention:**
- Note prominent landmarks continuously
- Look back frequently (terrain looks different in reverse)
- Estimate travel speed and time
- Stay oriented to major terrain features
- If uncertain, STOP — don't compound the problem

**If Lost:**
1. **STOP** — Sit down, stay calm
2. **THINK** — Where did you last know your location?
3. **OBSERVE** — What landmarks are visible?
4. **PLAN** — Best course of action (stay put vs. navigate out)

## When to Stay Put vs. Move

**Stay Put If:**
- People know where you are and expect your return
- You're injured or exhausted
- Weather is poor
- Night is falling
- You're completely disoriented

**Move If:**
- No one knows your location
- You can see clear route to safety (road, town, river to follow)
- Staying puts you in danger (avalanche zone, flood area)
- You have map and compass and know where you are

## Urban and Developed Area Navigation

- Follow power lines (lead to civilization)
- Follow streams downhill (lead to larger water, often settlements)
- Head toward sounds of traffic, trains, or aircraft patterns
- Look for light glow on night horizon (indicates towns)

**Remember:** In survival navigation, approximate direction toward safety is better than precise direction to nowhere. Priority is getting out alive, not arriving at exact GPS coordinates.
